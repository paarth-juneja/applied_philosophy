The Structure of Reasoning

The Art of Reasoning

Logic is the study of correct reasoning. It's not concerned with *what* we think, but *how* we think. It examines the structure of arguments to distinguish valid reasoning from fallacious reasoning, providing the tools for clear and defensible thought.

Arguments, Premises, and Conclusions: In logic, an 'argument' is not a dispute but a set of statements. It consists of 'premises' (supporting claims) that are intended to provide reasons for accepting a 'conclusion' (the main claim). For example: "All BITSians are Indians (premise). All Indians are honest (premise). Therefore, all BITSians are honest (conclusion)."

Deductive and Inductive Reasoning: There are two main types of reasoning. Deductive reasoning moves from general principles to a specific conclusion. If the premises are true, the conclusion *must* be true. This provides certainty. Inductive reasoning moves from specific observations to a general conclusion. For example, "Every swan I have ever seen is white. Therefore, all swans are white." This provides probability, not certainty, because the next observation could prove the conclusion false (as happened when black swans were discovered).

Questions & Answers: Logical Perspectives

Test your knowledge of the key concepts from Logic.

Q1: What is the primary focus of logic?

  • Logic is the study of the principles of correct reasoning.
  • Its primary focus is on the structure and validity of arguments, distinguishing good reasoning from flawed reasoning (fallacies).

Q2: What is the key difference between a deductive and an inductive argument?

  • Deductive Argument: Aims for certainty. It moves from general premises to a specific conclusion. If the premises are true, the conclusion is guaranteed to be true. (Example: All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore, Socrates is mortal.)
  • Inductive Argument: Aims for probability. It moves from specific observations to a general conclusion. The conclusion is likely, but not guaranteed, to be true even if the premises are true. (Example: The sun has risen every day so far. Therefore, the sun will rise tomorrow.)
