Democratic Values & The Internet

Technology as a Tool for Freedom

The Internet and Democratic Values

Democracy ("rule of the people") is a form of government built on the values of justice, equality, and freedom. The internet, as a technology, is fundamentally an outcome of the value of freedom. Its open, affordable, and decentralized nature can be a powerful tool for supporting democracy.

Benefits for Democracy

  • Freedom of Expression: Allows citizens to challenge and criticize power, a basic democratic right.
  • Transparency: By putting public data online, governments can become more transparent and accountable.
  • Informed Citizens: Free and open access to online news helps people become better informed on current affairs.
  • Political Participation: Social media can help organize and bring about democratic change.

Troublesome Issues

  • Accessibility: The digital divide means not everyone has equal access.
  • Data Security: Concerns over data storage, security, and violation of privacy.
  • Misinformation: The spread of "fake news" and the difficulty of verifying data.
  • Intolerance: The internet can also be a platform for intolerance, not just open debate.

Case Studies: The Internet & Well-Being

Case 1: The "Harmless" University Virus

Summary: Your friend is creating a "harmless" worm that will display "Let's party!" on all university workstations on a Friday at 3 pm, then erase itself by 4 pm. What would you say or do?

The Core Dilemma: This is a conflict between loyalty to a friend (a personal value) and the ethical duty to prevent harm (a social value). Is a "harmless" prank that involves unauthorized access still a wrongful act?

Philosophical Analysis:

  • Deontology (Kant): The friend's action is fundamentally immoral. His maxim would be, "It is acceptable to gain unauthorized access to a computer system if my intention is harmless." This cannot be universalized; if everyone did this, the very concepts of security, privacy, and property would collapse. He is also treating the university (and its IT staff, who will have to investigate) as a *mere means* to his end (a prank).
  • Utilitarianism (Mill): The friend believes his action will produce a small amount of "pleasure" (a party message) and no "pain" (it's "harmless"). This is a bad calculation. The potential negative consequences are huge: the worm could have an unforeseen bug, it could crash critical systems (like exam databases or research projects), and it will cause panic and waste the time of IT staff. The potential pain (high) far outweighs the potential pleasure (low).

My Conclusion: The action is unethical. I would have a moral duty to intervene. The first step would be to try to persuade my friend not to do it, using the arguments above. If he refused, I would be ethically obligated to report him to the university administration. The Kantian duty to uphold the rules of a just system (respect for property and security) and the utilitarian duty to prevent a large, foreseeable harm outweigh my personal loyalty to my friend.

Case 2 & 3: Bedtime Media & Facebook for Anxiety

Summary: One study finds that *long* media use before bed is associated with less sleep. However, *short, focused* media use (like listening to music) in bed is associated with *more* sleep. A second study finds that students who read supportive "likes" and comments on Facebook before an exam reduced their anxiety and performed better.

The Core Dilemma: Is technology an inherent source of anxiety and distraction, or is it a neutral tool whose impact depends entirely on *how* we use it?

Philosophical Analysis:

  • Aristotelian Virtue (Virtue Ethics): These cases are a perfect example of Aristotle's "Golden Mean." Technology use is not a simple good or bad. The *excess* (long, multitasking media use) is a vice that leads to less sleep. The *deficiency* (no media) might be fine, but the *mean* (a short, focused, purposeful session) is a virtue that can actually improve well-being.
  • Utilitarianism (Welfare): These cases show how to maximize welfare using IT. The goal is to maximize pleasure (reduced anxiety, better sleep) and minimize pain (anxiety, insomnia). The Facebook study is a clear utilitarian win: a small, low-cost action (posting for support) produced a significant increase in well-being and performance for a large group (anxious students).

My Conclusion: These studies demonstrate that technology is not a monolithic good or bad. It is a powerful tool that must be used with virtue and wisdom. The "Bedtime" case shows that *temperance* is key—using media in moderation and with a clear purpose. The "Facebook" case shows that IT can be a powerful tool for fostering "social belonging," a key indicator of quality of life. The impact of IT is not in the technology; it's in our *habits*.
