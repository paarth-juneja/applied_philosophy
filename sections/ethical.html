Inquiries into the Good Life

How Should We Live?

Ethics, or moral philosophy, is the branch that explores questions of right and wrong, good and evil. It seeks to answer fundamental questions like "How should I live a good life?" and "What are my duties to others?" Three of the most influential approaches come from Aristotle, John Stuart Mill, and Immanuel Kant.

Aristotle's Virtue Ethics: For Aristotle, the goal of life is 'eudaimonia' or human flourishing. This is achieved not by following strict rules, but by cultivating a virtuous character. A virtue is a "golden mean" between two extremes. For example, courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness. By practicing virtues, we develop a good character and learn to make the right choices naturally.

Mill's Utilitarianism: John Stuart Mill was a key proponent of Utilitarianism. This is a consequentialist theory, meaning it judges an action's morality based on its outcome. The core principle is the "greatest happiness principle": the right action is the one that produces the greatest amount of happiness for the greatest number of people. It requires a calculation of potential pleasures and pains resulting from a decision.

Kant's Deontology: Immanuel Kant proposed a deontological (duty-based) ethical theory. He argued that morality is not about consequences but about intention and duty. The moral worth of an action comes from acting out of respect for the moral law. He formulated this law as the "Categorical Imperative," with one version stating: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law." In other words, if you cannot rationally wish for everyone to act the way you are acting, then your action is morally wrong.

Questions & Answers: Ethical Perspectives

Test your knowledge of the key concepts from Ethics.

Q1: Contrast the ethical theories of J.S. Mill and Immanuel Kant.

  • Mill's Utilitarianism: This is a consequentialist theory. The morality of an action is judged solely by its outcome or consequences. The right action is the one that maximizes overall happiness for the greatest number of people.
  • Kant's Deontology: This is a duty-based theory. The morality of an action is judged by the intention behind it, not its consequences. An action is only moral if it is done out of respect for a universal moral law (the Categorical Imperative), regardless of the outcome.

Q2: What is Aristotle's concept of the "golden mean"?

  • The "golden mean" is the core of Aristotle's virtue ethics.
  • He believed that every virtue is a midpoint between two extremes (vices) of deficiency and excess.
  • For example, the virtue of courage is the mean between the deficiency of cowardice and the excess of recklessness. Living a good life involves finding this balance in all aspects of one's character.
